Op Ed

Author: Mary Lycke, MPA, MSBA

Since the 2023 Supreme Court ruling of Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard (SFFA) found that affirmative action in most college admissions is unconstitutional. I’ve been keen to examine how this landmark decision would impact admissions for my own institution, Stony Brook University, as well as for other universities across the state. Despite this change in the law, I want to continue growing an academic community at Stony Brook that is diverse, representing the racial and ethnic composition of New York State. Whether or not affirmative action programs help, hinder, or have no noticeable impact on this valued diversity is the matter at hand.

Known for its STEM programs, Stony Brook is one of the largest schools in the SUNY system. Nestled on the north shore of Long Island, Stony Brook is home to around 18,000 undergraduate and 8,500 graduate students. A significant portion of our student body is international, with an average percentage of about 12% international students over the past ten years. In 2024, about 25% of the undergraduate student body was white, 6% black, 16% hispanic, 36% asian, 10% international, and the remaining being a mix of Native American, two or more races, or unknown.

The demographics at Stony Brook have changed quite a bit between 2010 and 2024, with white first-year undergraduate students being 37% of the incoming freshman in 2010 compared to 18.5% in 2024. On the other hand, there was an increase of 7.2 percentage points for black and hispanic freshman between 2010 and 2024.

For both undergraduate and graduate enrollment, there was a similar shift in the enrollment, with white students making up 41.8% of the total study body population in 2010 and a steady decrease over the next 15 years. White students made up only 29.2% of total student enrollment in 2024. There wasn’t a meaningful increase in the black population of the study body during this time period, but there are notable increases in hispanic and asian populations between 2010 and 2024.

To get an idea on the potential impact of the Supreme Court ruling on Stony Brook’s demographics, I pulled data from University of California - Santa Cruz, a California public university with a similar sized undergraduate student body. Since public institutions of higher education in California were specifically not allowed to implement Affirmative Action programs even before the SFFA decision, a California public university with a similar sized student body would help evaluate impact.

My findings, however, were interesting mainly because of how uninteresting they were. Both institutions show a slight decrease in white and Asian enrollment in 2024, with non-white and non-Asian groups seeing an all-time high in 2024 at Stony Brook. So far, it appears SFFA has not had much of an impact on preventing non-white and non-Asian individuals from gaining access to higher education in the United States.

American society has come a long way since its founding, and our history is riddled with examples of us taking two steps towards equity and one step back. There inevitably will be new factors that will impact university demographics in the coming years, but it will still be imperative to monitor these changes post-2023 to truly understand how SFFA influenced the student bodies of American universities. Whether or not the SFFA decision has further disadvantaged non-white and non-Asian individuals from gaining access to higher education is yet to be seen, but thankfully, so far it seems that there are other factors at hand in bridging equity gaps than just affirmative action.
